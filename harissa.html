Kashmiri Harissa
Non-Vegetarian | Dinner | Lunch
Ingredients
For the meat base:
  • 1 kg mutton (with bones, preferably shank or leg pieces)
  • ¼ cup rice (preferably short-grain)
  • 1½ tbsp fennel powder (saunf)
  • 1 tsp dry ginger powder (shonth)
  • 1 tsp cumin seeds (jeera)
  • 3–4 green cardamoms (elaichi)
  • 1 black cardamom (badi elaichi)
  • 2–3 cloves (laung)
  • 1 small piece cinnamon (dalchini)
  • 1 bay leaf (tej patta)
  • Salt to taste
  • 1 pinch asafoetida (hing)
  • 4–5 cups water
    • For finishing:
  • ½ cup ghee (traditional) or oil
  • 1 tsp Kashmiri red chili powder (optional, for colour)
    • Optional garnishes:
  • Fried onions (for serving)
  • Crispy lamb bits or kebabs (served alongside in traditional harissa meals)
    • Method
  • Prepare the spice-infused meat stock

    -In a heavy-bottomed pot, add mutton pieces, bay leaf, cinnamon, cardamoms, cloves, cumin seeds, hing, fennel powder, dry ginger powder, and salt.

    -Pour in 4–5 cups of water.

    -Bring to a boil, then simmer on low flame until the meat becomes tender (about 1½–2 hours).

  • Add rice for thickening

    -Wash and add rice to the pot once the meat is soft.

    -Cook further until rice is completely mushy and mixed into the broth.

  • Mash and blend

    -Remove bones carefully.

    -Using a wooden spoon (masala khund) or hand masher, pound and mash the meat-rice mixture until it becomes smooth and paste-like.

    -You can also use a hand blender for a finer consistency (optional modern method).

  • Cook the harissa paste

    -Continue to cook on very low heat, stirring constantly to avoid sticking.

    -In a separate pan, heat ghee and add chili powder (if using).

    -Pour this hot ghee over the harissa and mix well.

  • Final slow cooking (dum)

    -Cover the pot and let it cook on low flame for 15–20 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the harissa turns rich, thick, and glossy.

    • Serving

    Serve hot with fresh Naan

    Traditional Harissa is a winter breakfast dish — slow-cooked overnight to achieve a velvety texture and deep flavour — often enjoyed with a cup of Nunchai (salted tea).
